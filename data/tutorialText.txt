Breaking Caesar’s Cipher-

During wartime, Julius Caesar developed a simple encryption method to send secret messages. In this cipher, each letter in a message is shifted forward in the alphabet by a fixed number of positions. The shift can range from 1 to 25 letters.

For example, using a shift of 3:

“cat in the hat” becomes “fdw lq wkh kdw.”

Only someone who knows the shift value can easily decrypt the message.

Approximately 800 years later, the mathematician Al-Kindi identified a fundamental weakness in this system. He observed that written language follows predictable patterns. In large bodies of text, certain letters appear more frequently than others, creating a consistent frequency distribution.

By analyzing the frequency of letters in an encrypted message and comparing it to known language patterns, it becomes possible to estimate the shift and break the cipher.
